A History of Broadway

In 1750, Thomas Kean and Walter Murray opened one of the first theatres in New York City on Nassau Street. It was large enough to hold 280 patrons. Patrons flocked newly established theaters to see such ballad operas as John Gay's The Beggar's Opera and Shakespearean plays like The Merchant of Venice.

  • The Beggar's Opera
  • The Merchant Of Venice

Early theater in New York was separated according to class. Opera was attended by the wealthy. Melodramas and Minstrel shows were popular among the middle class. The working class went to more affordable variety shows. Musicals weren't performed until the mid 19th century. Prior to this, performances were non-musical acts. Considered the first modern musical, The Black Crook came out in 1866 and contained both original music and choreographed dance. It was a little less than six hours long. Comic Operas by Gilbert and Sullivan were also extremely popular during that time.

Tin Pan Alley

(1890-1930)

Popular music met big business with big results. In Tin Pan Alley (28th Street between Broadway and Fifth Avenue in New York City) numerous music publishing houses poured forth popular songs each year. The musical theater and the private parlor rang with the sounds of ragtime, romantic ballads, and comedy songs.

Famous composers we often associate with Broadway - Cole Porter, Rodgers and Hart, George Gershwin, etc. - began composing music for Broadway shows during the "raging" 1920's.

Broadway hosts many of the best actors from all over the world. Various Award ceremonies were established to recognize their efforts. The Tony Awards, established in 1947, highlight those who invested their hearts and souls into theater. More specific to Broadway, the Drama Desk Awards are given to Broadway and off-Broadway productions.

Controversy

The Cradle will Rock, directed by Orson Welles, was put on despite:

  1. The doors to the theater were chained shut and under guard four days before opening night.
  2. Actors could not appear on any unsanctioned stage (actors' union)
  3. They would have to pay the musicians their full rate instead of the reduced FTP rate, as well as back pay for previous rehearsals, and they would also have to hire more musicians to comply with standard Broadway contracts. (musicians' union)
  4. The actors were unable to access any of their costumes, props, or set pieces.

Solution: The audience was dragged to a different theater, the actors perform while hidden amongst said audience, and the composer himself accompanied on piano.

Result: The show was a hit!
