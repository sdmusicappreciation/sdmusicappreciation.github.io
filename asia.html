Japan

Neo-Traditional

Traditional Japanese music came from the theatre, court, or daily lives of the commonfolk. The range of instruments was fairly limited, including the voice, taiko (drum), Nohkan (flute), shamisen (three string “guitar”), koto (zither), and the shakuhachi (a flute that became very popular in the Edo era).

However, traditional music has been kept alive and partially modernized through the efforts of some insanely talented people. The Yoshida Brothers are masters of the shamisen who have popularized the music around the world. Wagakki Band has created engaging rock music using almost exclusively traditional instruments.

After the US forced Japan to open to trade their music began to change rapidly. It will be impossible to cover all the varied changes it underwent and the paths it followed, but we’ll stick to the more well known and popularized forms of 20th century Japanese music.

Rekuhkara (レクッカラ)

The Ainu are the indigenous people of northern Japan. They've managed to keep their native musical forms alive throughout much of the 20th century in spite of pogroms carried out against them by the imperial Japanese (very similar to how the United States and Canada have carried out a campaign of genocide against Native American Indians).

While there are many aspects to, and forms of, Ainu traditional music, here I’d like to highlight Rekuhkara. The last “true” performer of Rekuhkara passed in the 70s, but others have worked to keep it alive.

Rekuhkara (alternatively written rekuxkara or rekukkara) is a form of throat-singing performed by two women, where the women connect their mouths with their hands and one woman creates the sounds into the other woman’s mouth and the second woman adjusts her throat and mouth to modulate the sound. This is decidedly not COVID safe.

Ryukyuan

Ryukyuan music is the music of the indigenous people of southern Japan. Similar to the Ainu, there’s far too much to go into, so for this lesson I’d like to share just a little bit of their music. The instruments sound fairly similar to their mainland Japanese counterparts (due to heavy trade between Japan and China via the Ryukyu kingdom), but the vocal style is distinctly Ryukyuan, even noticeable in the singing of more pop oriented performers from that region today. Ryukoka/Kayokyoku (流行歌・歌謡曲)

The 1920s saw the split of Japanese music into two sub-genres, Ryukoka (“popular song”) and Kayokyoku (“pop song”). That doesn’t seem like a huge distinction and, truth be told, in practice it isn’t.

Ryukoka can technically be used to refer to any “popular” music stretching back over a thousand years. More colloquially, it refers to the popular music of the early 20th century that was less influenced by Western musical forms. Its relevance largely died out after the 30s.

Kayokyoku, on the other hand, was much more influenced by Western music. In my opinion, it’s not quite right to call kayokyoku a genre, since its only defining feature is its influence. Kayokyoku covers such music forms as simple ballads, rock’n’roll, and all-out pop.

All that said, there’s a ton of overlap in the two terms, to the point that they were even used interchangeably for much of their history.

Enka (演歌)

Enka is interesting since it's a much newer form of music and was quite popular (still is, in some circles), yet it echoes traditional Japanese music much more than the older popular forms.

Enka developed in the post-war era as an emotionally fraught ballad genre that brought back into the mainstream the kobushi form of singing, where individual syllables are elongated and brought through several different pitches.

While it reached the peak of its popularity during the Showa era it has by no means died, and is being kept alive today by the new and old generation alike even hitting the top 10 charts now and then.

City Pop

This genre had a brief tenure, lasting only from the late 70s through the 80s. While it’s known as City Pop in the West (and is sorta known by that in Japan), it was mostly understood by the moniker “New Music.”

It’s hard to pinpoint what exactly certifies something as “City Pop,” but it was very much a music of the economic boom, meant to be played in fancy new walkmans and shiny new cars as you moved through the city. You’ll feel intricate basslines, luxurious vocals, and a warm harmonizing of disco, funk, jazz, & pop.

City Pop has seen a massive resurgence in popularity… but not in Japan. At some point in the last few years, the YouTube algorithm started recommending City Pop to a ton of people who’d never heard it before (I was one of those people). Now fans from all over the world have fallen in love with this older form of music.

This is a big surprise to the Japanese themselves, who basically forgot about it once the 90s rolled around. Ask a random older Japanese person who Mariya Takeuchi is and they’ll happily recount their memories of listening to her with their friends in their youth. But, ask about her single, “Plastic Love” and you’ll get blank stares.

You see, while Takeuchi was a huge star, “Plastic Love” was a B-side; however, that rascally YouTube algorithm doesn’t know the difference and now a new generation of City Pop lovers have pushed previously unappreciated songs to new heights of popularity.

Anime

I’ve always struggled thinking of Anime music as a genre of its own. And, really, it’s not. Theme songs for anime can be from any genre. However, due to the ubiquity and cultural significance of anime in Japan, certain songs have ingrained themselves into the culture in rather unique ways. Each song is crafted to reflect the themes of the show, to engage and excite the viewer and prepare them to enter the fictional world they've sat in front of. They're an integral part of anime. And anime is an integral part of modern Japan.

The closest examples I can think of would be how an American might react to hearing the Andy Griffith Show whistle, or the Full House theme song. These songs are in a category of their own because their connection to the anime art form is a part of the very fabric of the Japanese musical culture.

Cambodia

In the late 50s, Norodom Sihanouk, the head of state, pushed the country to develop a home-grown popular music scene. What came out of it was an absolutely incredible rock’n’roll scene. It’s believed that most of the artists of that era were killed during the bloody Khmer Rouge genocide. However, in 1996, 22 tracks were collected onto a single album—titled “Cambodian Rocks”—and released to the world. That’s what we’ll be listening to a little bit of today. It’s a stunning melding of Western rock’n’roll with the unique, local Cambodian sound. One of my favorite rock’n’roll records to listen to!

Indonesia

For Indonesia we’re going to feature the traditional ensemble music form known as Gamelan, performed by the Balinese, Javanese, and Sudanese people on the archipelago. Of course, in keeping with the theme of the class, we’ll be investigating the modern, updated forms of this traditional music.

According to myth, the first gamelan was organized around 230 CE. A complete gamelan consists of several instruments:

  • Kendang - two headed drum used to keep the beat
  • Kemanak - banana shaped bronze instrument, struck to produce a sound
  • Gangsa - Very reminiscent of a metal xylophone
  • Rebab - one of the earliest known bowed instruments in the world!
  • Siter - Basically a zither
  • Sindhen - female singer
  • Gerong - male chorus

There are a lot more. At least 3 dozen, and most seem to be varieties of xylophones which I’m sure would take many lessons to learn the ins-and-outs of. The ones I’ve listed are the ones that (I confess) Wikipedia suggests are the most common. . To list them all, and all the arrangements of them would keep you scrolling until your fingers fell off! Needless to say, it's a very deep and elaborate art form!

Mongolia

Mongolian music is world famous for its overtone throat singing. Traditionally, it also featured two really distinct things. One is the morin khuur which is… well, I guess not that distinct. It’s a bowed instrument. The really unique thing is the Urtyin duu, or “long song.” In these songs, individual syllables are stretched out to incredible lengths, such that a 4 minute song could only have ten words in it!

In modern times, popular music has exploded in the relatively small country of roughly 3.2 million people. They’ve got boy bands, girl groups, alternative, hard rock, rock’n’roll, heavy metal, EDM, and hip-hop--and it all flourishes in the capital of Ulaanbaatar. Additionally, there have been some exceptionally successful groups that have modernized the traditional music for 21st century audiences and made it rock.
