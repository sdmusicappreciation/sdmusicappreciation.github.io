Vallenato

Vallenato, meaning "born in the valley," came out of the pastures of the Regi√≥n Caribe in Colombia. The herders, moving cattle through the towns, would carry news from place to place, conveying these stories through song via singing, guitars, and the indigenous gaita flutes (aka kuisis).

Over the years it developed, bringing in new instruments to compliment its complicating sound. The cane-like guachara scratches out a percussive sound; the caja is a small drum that originated with African slaves; and eventually the charming accordion joined the musical fray.

Four rhythms tied to an accompanying chord pattern make up the structure of vallenato music, usually dictated by the accordion. Son is mournful and slow, heavily accentuated with stress on the low notes of the accordion. Paseo is a slightly more upbeat version of Son, and is probably the most widely recorded. Finally, Puya is an elaboration of Merengue, increasing the speed and giving solos to each of the instruments.

Cumbia brings together all the cultures of Colombia, native, Spanish, and African.
